# HR Scout — Calibration & Market Validation

**Headline: No. HR Scout does *not* beat betting-market consensus.** But it is **excellently calibrated on its own distribution** — once scores are converted to probabilities, the predicted HR rate matches the actual rate within ~0.5% in every score band. Its value is **honest probability display and tier sorting, not a betting edge.**

Generated by `scripts/calibrate.py` + `scripts/validate_market.py` on the full-season relabeled `public/history.json`.

---

## Data foundation

- **Full season, relabeled** after the name-matcher fix in `scripts/update_results.py`. The old last-name-only fallback cross-credited home runs across every shared surname (when any "Smith"/"Jr." homered, *every* Smith/Jr. in the pool was marked a hit), inflating Jr./II players to a phantom **32%** hit rate. The fix requires suffix-stripped **last name + first initial + team** to all agree on the disambiguating path.
- **Coverage: Apr 1 – Jul 9, 2026 — 98 days, 21,095 scored player-games.**
- **Base HR rate: 10.13%.** After the fix, Jr./II players sit at **10.1%**, identical to everyone else (10.1%) — the phantom inflation is gone.
- **Independent label validation:** every player also appearing in the betting file `analysis/hr_favorites_odds_picks.csv` on the same date (n=580) was cross-checked against that file's independent `homered` column → **0 disagreements, 0 false positives, 0 false negatives.** The labels are trustworthy.

## Calibration (score → P(HR))

Single-feature logistic regression, fit by Newton/IRLS (no sklearn):

```
P(HR) = sigmoid(-4.6429 + 0.3734 · score)
```

| coefficient | value | SE |
|---|---|---|
| intercept (b0) | −4.6429 | 0.1937 |
| score (b1) | +0.3734 | 0.0288 |

Odds ratio per +1 score point = **1.453×** (b1 is ~13 SE from zero — highly significant).

### Bucket table (actual vs. model)

| score band | n | HR | actual rate | model P | lift vs base |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| < 5 | 319 | 16 | 0.050 | 0.055 | 0.50 |
| 5–6 | 5,265 | 393 | 0.075 | 0.074 | 0.74 |
| 6–7 | 10,330 | 1,011 | 0.098 | 0.098 | 0.97 |
| 7–8 | 4,392 | 574 | 0.131 | 0.132 | 1.29 |
| 8–9 | 745 | 132 | 0.177 | 0.177 | 1.75 |
| 9–10 | 44 | 10 | 0.227 | 0.234 | 2.24 |

Model prediction tracks the empirical rate to **within ~0.5% in every band**, and the gradient is monotone from 5.0% → 22.7%. **Calibration and tier sorting both work.**

### Brier score (calibrated model vs. base-rate-only)

| predictor | Brier |
|---|---|
| calibrated model | **0.09026** |
| base-rate-only (everyone = 10.13%) | 0.09100 |
| **Brier skill score** | **+0.81%** |

The model is well calibrated but adds only modest Brier skill over "predict the league rate for everyone" — expected, since most players sit in the 6–7 band near the base rate. The value is in **which tier to trust**, not sharp per-player separation.

Lookup table (`public/calibration.json`) exposes `intercept`/`coef` and a score→pHR table (0.0–12.0, step 0.1) so the frontend can display a real probability next to each score. Examples: score 6.0 → 8.2%, 8.0 → 16.4%, 10.0 → 28.9%.

## Market validation (the key test)

Joined the calibrated model to `analysis/hr_favorites_odds_picks.csv` (the shortest-priced HR favorite per game) on (date, normalized name).

- Overlap: **580 picks across 96 days** (157 actual HRs; hit rate **27.1%** — far above the 10% pool rate, because these are the market's *favorites*).
- This is a deliberately **high-end, adversarial subset**: it tests the model exactly where the market is most confident. It is the fair test, but not a test of the model across the broad pool.

### (a) Brier score on the overlap set (n=580)

| predictor | Brier |
|---|---|
| **market** (consensus_implied) | **0.1988** |
| base-rate-only (27.1%) | 0.1974 |
| **model** (calibrated P) | **0.2132** |

- **The market wins**, and the model is worse than even a flat base-rate predictor on this subset.
- Paired bootstrap (10k resamples): 95% CI of (model − market) Brier diff = **[+0.0032, +0.0258]** (entirely positive → model worse); **P(model better) = 0.53%.** This is a robust result, not noise.
- **Why:** mean model P on these picks = **0.145** vs actual **0.271**. The model **systematically under-predicts** the market's favorites. Its calibrated ceiling (~18% even at score 8–9) sits far below where elite sluggers (Ohtani, Judge, Schwarber, Alvarez, Marte) homer in favorable spots. The logistic compresses the top end.

### (b) Model's top-3 daily picks vs. their market implied

Of the model's top-3-by-score each day, 98 player-days also had a market price:

| metric | value |
|---|---|
| actual HR hit rate | 0.286 (28/98) |
| mean market implied | 0.308 |
| delta | **−0.022 (did NOT beat)** |

The model's own best picks hit **at, slightly below, their market-implied probability** — no edge.

### (c) Biggest model-vs-market disagreements

Every large disagreement is the same shape: **model lower, market higher** (~0.10–0.18 vs ~0.32–0.42), always on elite sluggers.

| date | player | mkt | model | gap | HR? | closer |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 04-03 | Shohei Ohtani | 0.39 | 0.11 | −0.28 | YES | market |
| 06-30 | Kyle Schwarber | 0.39 | 0.13 | −0.26 | no | model |
| 05-22 | Aaron Judge | 0.36 | 0.10 | −0.26 | no | model |
| 07-07 | Kyle Schwarber | 0.37 | 0.11 | −0.25 | YES | market |
| 06-13 | Shea Langeliers | 0.42 | 0.18 | −0.24 | no | model |
| 06-30 | Manny Machado | 0.34 | 0.10 | −0.23 | YES | market |
| 04-06 | Shohei Ohtani | 0.38 | 0.14 | −0.24 | YES | market |

> **Do not be fooled by the raw count** ("model closer on 417 of 571 disagreements"). That is a **base-rate artifact**, not skill: the model always predicts lower, so whenever the player *doesn't* homer (~73% of the time) the lower number is trivially closer to 0. 417/571 = 73% ≈ the no-HR rate. When the player *does* homer — the outcome the market's higher price is paying for — the **market is closer nearly every time**. The proper scoring rule (Brier, panel (a)) already accounts for this, and it says market.

### Statistical meaningfulness

- Overlap n = 580, 157 HR events. SE of the overlap hit rate ≈ **0.018** (±0.036 at 95%).
- The model-vs-market Brier gap (0.0144) is comfortably outside that noise floor, and the bootstrap CI is entirely on the "market better" side. **This conclusion is statistically meaningful**, not a small-sample fluke.
- The market itself is only marginally better than the in-sample base rate (0.1988 vs 0.1974) and slightly *overprices* favorites (mean implied 0.293 vs actual 0.271 — the usual vig/favorite bias). The market isn't a genius; the model is just worse at the top end.

## What this means

1. **The model is well-calibrated on its own distribution.** Convert a score to a probability and it is honest — the displayed "19%" means what it says, validated across 21,000+ player-games.
2. **It does not beat closing-line consensus.** On the games where the two can be compared, the model is less accurate (higher Brier), systematically under-prices favorites, and its top picks land at — not above — market. Bootstrap P(model better) = 0.53%.
3. **It compresses the top end.** The single-score logistic caps out ~25–30% even for elite sluggers the market prices at 35–42%. Even a top-end recalibration would, on this evidence (panel b), at best *match* the market, not beat it.
4. **The honest use of HR Scout is probability *display* and *tier sorting*, not betting edge.** Surfacing the calibrated P(HR) next to each score tells the user, truthfully, how likely a HR is — while making no claim to out-predict the market. Any betting value would have to come from games the market doesn't price a favorite for, which this dataset cannot test.

**Data limits:** one partial season (Apr–Jul 2026), single market snapshot per game. Revisit as more season accrues.
